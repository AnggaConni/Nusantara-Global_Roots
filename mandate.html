The Big Picture

More than labels.
Living mandates.

Indonesia is among the most extraordinary nations on Earth — a megadiverse archipelago of over 17,000 islands, home to more than 270 million people, 700 spoken languages, and some of the planet's most irreplaceable ecosystems. Protecting this richness requires more than good intentions.

International designations are legally grounded, operationally active frameworks. They oblige communities, governments, and civil society organisations to uphold agreed standards of stewardship. They unlock international technical cooperation, funding mechanisms, and peer-reviewed monitoring. For an NGO like Nusantara — Global Roots, they are the architecture within which meaningful, scalable action becomes possible.

Below is a comprehensive guide to each designation — what it is, how it works, why it matters, and what it demands of all who operate under its umbrella.

World Heritage

Convention Concerning the Protection of the World Cultural and Natural Heritage

"The heritage of remarkable universal value which belongs, not to one nation, but to all of humanity."

Est. 1972 UNESCO Convention
Sites 1,200+ worldwide; 9 in Indonesia
Types Cultural, Natural, Mixed
Body World Heritage Committee
Review Periodic reporting cycle

What is World Heritage?

The 1972 Convention Concerning the Protection of the World Cultural and Natural Heritage was born from a simple but profound recognition: some places are so exceptional that their loss would impoverish all of humanity, not merely the nation in which they reside. A World Heritage Site carries Outstanding Universal Value (OUV) — a concept defined by a set of ten criteria against which every site is rigorously evaluated.

The Ten Criteria for Outstanding Universal Value

Criteria (i)–(vi) are cultural; (vii)–(x) are natural. A site must meet at least one criterion and demonstrate integrity, authenticity (for cultural sites), and an effective protection and management system.

(i)

Masterpiece of human creative genius — such as Borobudur's extraordinary architectural achievement

(ii)

Important interchange of human values — evidence of the cross-cultural exchange of ideas

(iii)

Unique testimony to a civilisation — bearing witness to a living or vanished cultural tradition

(iv)

Outstanding example of architecture — illustrating a significant stage in human history

(v)

Outstanding example of human settlement — especially where it has become vulnerable under change

(vi)

Directly associated with living traditions or beliefs — of universal significance

(vii)

Exceptional natural beauty and aesthetic importance

(viii)

Outstanding geological history — major stages of Earth's development

(ix)

Significant ecological and biological processes — in terrestrial, freshwater, or marine ecosystems

(x)

Significant natural habitats for biodiversity conservation — including threatened species

World Heritage in Indonesia

Indonesia is a signatory to the World Heritage Convention and currently holds 9 inscribed sites — a figure that reflects both the country's extraordinary natural endowment and the depth of its civilisational history. Each site carries specific obligations: a management plan, buffer zones, and periodic reporting to the World Heritage Committee.

Cultural · Criteria (i)(ii)(vi)
Borobudur Temple Compounds

The world's largest Buddhist monument. Built in the 9th century under the Sailendra dynasty, its 2,672 relief panels narrate the Buddhist path to enlightenment across 504 Buddha statues.

Cultural · Criteria (i)(ii)(iii)(iv)
Prambanan Temple Compounds

A 9th-century Hindu compound dedicated to the Trimurti — Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva — representing the pinnacle of classical Javanese Hindu architecture.

Cultural · Criteria (ii)(iii)(iv)(v)
Sangiran Early Man Site

One of the most important paleoanthropological sites in the world, Sangiran contains 50% of all known Homo erectus fossil findings, reshaping our understanding of human evolution.

Natural · Criteria (vii)(ix)(x)
Komodo National Park

Home to the Komodo dragon — the world's largest living lizard — and a marine environment of extraordinary biodiversity, including coral reefs, seagrass beds, and mangrove forests.

Natural · Criteria (vii)(ix)(x)
Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra

A cluster of three national parks — Gunung Leuser, Kerinci Seblat, and Bukit Barisan Selatan — sheltering the Sumatran orangutan, tiger, rhinoceros, and elephant.

Cultural · Criteria (ii)(iii)
Cultural Landscape of Bali

The Subak irrigation system — a philosophical, religious, and agricultural system representing the Balinese concept of Tri Hita Karana — harmony between the human, spiritual, and natural realms.

"The concept of outstanding universal value is the foundation upon which everything rests. To hold a World Heritage designation is to hold a public trust on behalf of all of humanity."

World Heritage Convention, 1972

Obligations & Management

Every site requires a State of Conservation report, a formally approved management plan, and active buffer zone management. Sites at risk may be placed on the List of World Heritage in Danger, triggering international assistance mechanisms — as occurred with the Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra due to deforestation pressures.

"The living heritage transmitted from generation to generation — constantly recreated by communities in response to their environment."

Est. 2003 UNESCO Convention
Elements 700+ worldwide; 13+ from Indonesia
Lists Representative List; Urgent Safeguarding List; Register of Best Practices
Body ICH Committee
Cycle 6-year periodic reporting

What is Intangible Cultural Heritage?

Where World Heritage protects physical places, the 2003 ICH Convention protects what cannot be touched: the living expressions of culture that communities carry within themselves. ICH is dynamic — it is constantly recreated, adapted, and transmitted. Critically, the Convention places communities, not states, at the centre of safeguarding decisions.

The Five Domains of ICH

The Convention identifies five broad domains within which living heritage manifests. Many elements span multiple domains simultaneously.

01

Oral traditions and expressions — including language as a vehicle of ICH (epics, poetry, riddles, proverbs, myths)

02

Performing arts — music, dance, theatre, puppetry, ritual performances

03

Social practices, rituals and festive events — ceremonies marking key moments in life and the agricultural calendar

04

Knowledge and practices concerning nature and the universe — traditional ecological knowledge, cosmologies, healing practices

05

Traditional craftsmanship — weaving, woodcarving, jewellery, metalwork, and other skills transmitted by artisans

Indonesia's Inscribed ICH Elements

Indonesia is one of the world's most richly represented countries on the ICH Representative List, reflecting the extraordinary cultural diversity of its 700+ ethnic groups. Each inscription carries a safeguarding plan co-developed with the relevant community and monitored over time.

Traditional Craftsmanship
Batik

Inscribed 2009. The art of wax-resist dyeing applied to cloth — a deeply layered cultural practice encoding philosophical meaning, identity, and occasion into every motif and colour choice.

Performing Arts
Wayang Puppet Theatre

Inscribed 2008. A sophisticated storytelling tradition drawing on the Hindu epics Mahabharata and Ramayana. The dalang (puppeteer) is simultaneously narrator, philosopher, and moral guide.

Performing Arts
Angklung

Inscribed 2010. A traditional musical instrument from West Java made of bamboo tubes. Its communal playing tradition embodies the Sundanese philosophy of gotong royong — collective harmony.

Traditional Craftsmanship
Noken — Multifunctional Knotted Bag

Inscribed 2012. The Noken of Papua is more than a bag — it is a symbol of peace, fertility, and the life force, woven exclusively by women and central to ceremonial exchange.

Performing Arts
Saman Dance

Inscribed 2011. A collective dance from the Gayo highlands of Aceh performed with extraordinary synchronicity. It serves as a medium for prayer, celebration, and the strengthening of communal bonds.

Traditional Craftsmanship · Nature
Pinisi — Art of Boatbuilding

Inscribed 2017. The Bugis and Makassarese peoples' mastery of crafting the pinisi sailing vessel — an extraordinary feat of traditional maritime engineering embedding celestial and ecological knowledge.

"ICH is the mirror of cultural diversity and a guarantee for sustainable development. It is not about freezing culture in amber — it is about ensuring communities have the means to transmit and recreate it."

UNESCO ICH Convention, 2003

The Distinction: Safeguarding vs. Preservation

The Convention deliberately uses the word safeguarding, not preservation. Preservation implies fixing something in its current form. Safeguarding means ensuring that the conditions for a living practice to continue, evolve, and be transmitted remain intact. This is a critical distinction — one that demands engagement with living communities rather than academic documentation alone.

Man and the Biosphere

UNESCO Man and the Biosphere Programme

"Living laboratories demonstrating that humans and nature can coexist and thrive in harmony."

Est. 1971 Intergovernmental Programme
Sites 740+ Biosphere Reserves in 134 countries; 18 in Indonesia
Network World Network of Biosphere Reserves (WNBR)
Review 10-year mandatory re-evaluation
Focus Conservation + sustainable use + knowledge

What is the MAB Programme?

Launched in 1971 as an intergovernmental scientific programme, MAB operates on a deceptively powerful premise: that the separation of humans and nature is a false one. Rather than designating areas from which people are excluded, MAB designates Biosphere Reserves — zones where human inhabitation, sustainable economic activity, and strict conservation operate in carefully managed layers, simultaneously.

Each Biosphere Reserve is structured around a three-zone model that allows different intensities of human activity while maintaining ecological integrity at its core.

Core Zone

Strictly protected ecosystem. Minimal human interference. Long-term biodiversity conservation and scientific monitoring.

Buffer Zone

Surrounds the core. Limited activities compatible with conservation: research, education, low-impact ecotourism.

Transition / Cooperation Zone

The outer zone where communities live and work. Sustainable agriculture, forestry, settlements, and cultural practices.

Three Functions, Simultaneously

Unlike protected areas focused solely on conservation, each Biosphere Reserve must fulfil three functions at once: Conservation (protecting biodiversity and landscapes), Development (fostering sustainable economic and human development), and Logistic support (research, monitoring, education, and knowledge exchange through the global WNBR network).

Indonesia's Biosphere Reserves

With 18 designated Biosphere Reserves, Indonesia is one of the most important national contributors to the World Network. These reserves span the extraordinary breadth of Indonesia's ecosystems — from the peat swamp forests of Kalimantan to the coral triangle of Sulawesi, and the highland systems of Papua.

Marine · Sulawesi Sea
Wakatobi Biosphere Reserve

Located in Southeast Sulawesi, Wakatobi sits at the heart of the Coral Triangle — the global centre of marine biodiversity. Its waters contain over 750 coral species, more than 940 reef fish species, and critical nesting habitat for green sea turtles.

Montane · West Java
Cibodas Biosphere Reserve

Established 1977 — one of the world's oldest MAB sites. Encompassing the upper slopes of the Gede-Pangrango massif, it protects crucial watershed functions for the greater Jakarta region and remains an active centre for botanical and ecological research.

Peat Swamp · Kalimantan
Berbak-Sembilang Biosphere Reserve

Among the most extensive areas of tropical peat swamp forest remaining in Southeast Asia. A critical carbon store, Sumatran tiger habitat, and nesting ground for millions of migratory shorebirds on the East Asian-Australasian Flyway.

Dryland · Nusa Tenggara
Lore Lindu Biosphere Reserve

Central Sulawesi. Notable for its exceptional level of endemism: 78% of mammal species and over 90% of freshwater fish found here occur nowhere else on Earth — a living testament to Wallacea's evolutionary isolation.

"Biosphere Reserves are not set-asides. They are lived-in landscapes where science and tradition, conservation and livelihoods, meet — and where solutions to the planet's most pressing challenges are worked out in practice."

UNESCO MAB Programme
UGGp

Global Geoparks

UNESCO Global Geoparks Programme

"Single, unified geographical areas where sites and landscapes of international geological significance are managed with a holistic concept of protection, education, and sustainable development."

Est. 2015 (formal UNESCO Programme)
Sites 200+ in 48 countries; 10 in Indonesia
Approach Bottom-up, community-led
Review 4-year revalidation cycle
Network Global Geoparks Network (GGN)

What is a UNESCO Global Geopark?

A UNESCO Global Geopark (UGGp) is a unified territory encompassing geological heritage of international significance — managed to promote protection, education, and sustainable regional development. Crucially, UGGp is the only UNESCO designation built explicitly on a bottom-up approach: communities themselves propose, develop, and manage the geopark, rather than receiving a designation imposed from above.

UGGp is notably not limited to geology in isolation. The programme explicitly recognises that geological heritage is inseparable from biological and cultural heritage. A landscape shaped by volcanic activity also shapes the people who farm its soils, the crops they grow, and the stories they tell.

The Three Pillars of UGGp Management

Every Geopark must demonstrably deliver on all three simultaneously: Conservation of geological, biological, and cultural heritage; Education through geotrails, interpretation centres, and school programmes; and Geotourism — sustainable tourism that generates local economic benefit and community pride. The 4-year revalidation is strict: failure to deliver leads to withdrawal of the designation.

Indonesia's UNESCO Global Geoparks

With 10 designated UGGps, Indonesia has rapidly become a significant player in the global network. The country's position at the meeting of three tectonic plates — the Eurasian, Indo-Australian, and Pacific — gives it an extraordinary variety of geological formations and processes, from active caldera systems to ancient metamorphic terrains.

Volcanic · Bali
Batur UNESCO Global Geopark

Centred on the Batur caldera complex — one of the largest calderas in Southeast Asia. The surrounding communities, led by the indigenous Bali Aga people, maintain a living relationship with the volcanic landscape expressed through ritual, agriculture, and architecture.

Mixed · West Java
Ciletuh-Palabuhanratu UGGp

Features some of the oldest exposed Cretaceous oceanic crust in Southeast Asia — 65 million-year-old ophiolite sequences — alongside waterfalls, rainforest, and traditional Sundanese fishing communities whose livelihoods are deeply tied to the bay.

Volcanic · North Sumatra
Toba Caldera UGGp

Lake Toba is the site of the largest known explosive volcanic eruption of the past 2 million years — a supervolcanic event 74,000 years ago that may have reduced the human global population to tens of thousands. Today it is home to the Batak people.

Karst · Central Java
Gunung Sewu UGGp

A classic conical karst landscape formed over millions of years. The name means "a thousand mountains" — an apt description of its 40,000+ limestone hills. It is also the source of some of Indonesia's most significant prehistoric human fossil findings.

Geoparks and Traditional Ecological Knowledge

UGGp explicitly recognises that local communities are not merely residents of geological sites — they are interpreters of them. In many Indonesian Geoparks, local mythology, agricultural practice, and community organisation reflect millennia of adaptation to volcanic and geological rhythms. Programmes within Geoparks actively document and integrate this knowledge alongside scientific geological interpretation.

What is the Creative Cities Network?

Established in 2004, UCCN brings together cities that have recognised culture and creativity as engines of sustainable urban development. It is the most urban-facing of all UNESCO designations — explicitly acknowledging that over 55% of the world's population lives in cities, and that culture must be central to how those cities plan, grow, and define themselves.

The network operates across seven creative fields, each representing a distinct cultural economy with its own dynamics, stakeholders, and developmental opportunities.

UCCN and the 2030 Agenda

UCCN is formally aligned with the UN 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, particularly SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities). The network's annual World Cities Day and the UCCN Annual Conference serve as key mechanisms for cities to share policy innovations in cultural planning, creative economy regulation, and inclusive urban governance.

Indonesia's Creative Cities

Indonesia currently has three cities in the UCCN network — each representing a distinct creative identity deeply rooted in local culture while projecting outward to a global creative economy.

Design · Inscribed 2015
Bandung — City of Design

Indonesia's third-largest city and its creative capital. Bandung's design scene emerged from a vibrant fashion and indie music culture in the 1990s, producing a generation of designers who have built successful global labels while drawing on Sundanese craft heritage. The city is now positioning design as a tool for urban regeneration and social innovation.

Music · Inscribed 2019
Ambon — City of Music

Known as the "City of Songs," Ambon's extraordinary musical culture reflects centuries of interaction between indigenous Maluku traditions, Portuguese colonial influences, and the broader Austronesian world. The city's churches, community ensembles, and street music festivals create a density of musical activity remarkable for a city of its size.

Literature · Inscribed 2021
Jakarta — City of Literature

As Indonesia's capital and most populous city, Jakarta holds a unique literary position as the meeting point of all of the archipelago's regional traditions — a city whose literature navigates urban alienation, colonial history, and multicultural identity in ways that resonate far beyond its borders. Its designation has catalysed new investment in libraries, literary festivals, and writing programmes for marginalised urban youth.

What This Means Locally
Beyond the Label — Policy Implications

UCCN membership requires cities to submit biennial reports demonstrating concrete actions taken under their creative city mandate. Cities must maintain at least 30% of their budget actions in cooperation with other network cities, fostering genuine transnational creative partnerships rather than a static title.

"The most sustainable city is not merely one that minimises environmental harm — it is one where citizens are active producers of culture, not passive consumers of it."

UNESCO Creative Cities Network, Mission Statement

The Complete Picture

How the five frameworks reinforce each other.

In practice, the most powerful conservation and development outcomes occur at the intersections — where multiple designations apply to the same territory or community, creating layered protection and layered opportunity.

Culture & Nature as Inseparable

The Balinese Subak irrigation system is simultaneously a World Heritage Site (Cultural Landscape) and embedded within traditional ecological knowledge that qualifies under ICH Domain IV. The volcanic geology of Mount Agung underpins both the Bali UGGp and the spiritual cosmology that defines Balinese ICH. These are not coincidences — they are the same reality, viewed through different analytical lenses.

WH ICH UGGp

Marine Ecosystems Across Designations

Indonesia's coral reef systems — particularly around the Coral Triangle — simultaneously qualify as World Heritage natural sites, are managed within MAB Biosphere Reserves (Wakatobi), and are the ecological base upon which coastal community ICH (maritime traditions, fishing rituals, boat-building knowledge) rests. Ocean Literacy — a core pillar of our work — bridges all three.

WH ICH MAB

Traditional Knowledge as Science

MAB's scientific mandate explicitly includes the documentation and application of traditional ecological knowledge (TEK). The same TEK documented under MAB's research framework is the living cultural knowledge safeguarded under the ICH Convention. In a Geopark, this knowledge becomes a community interpretation tool connecting geological history with cultural memory.

MAB ICH UGGp

Urban Creativity and Heritage

UCCN cities like Bandung and Jakarta are the urban destinations for artisans whose craft practices are inscribed as ICH. The sustainable creative economy UCCN promotes depends on authentic cultural production — which requires that the ICH from which it draws remains alive in communities. Creative cities are ICH safeguarding by another name.

UCCN ICH

Disaster Risk and Landscape Resilience

Many of Indonesia's most significant heritage landscapes sit within active geological hazard zones. The volcanic geology that makes Gunung Sewu a Geopark also creates seismic risk. MAB's transition zones are often the communities most exposed to flood and landslide. DRR — a core mandate of Nusantara — is not separate from heritage protection: resilient landscapes are the prerequisite for everything else.

MAB UGGp WH

Education as the Common Thread

Every single designation — WH, ICH, MAB, UGGp, UCCN — includes education as a core obligation, not an optional add-on. This is the space where our Education mandate is most powerful: developing curricula that integrate geological heritage with cultural practice, marine science with traditional seafaring knowledge, and urban creative economy with ICH safeguarding — simultaneously, for the same students.

Our Role

Where Nusantara operates within these frameworks.

We are not the designating authority — that is the role of intergovernmental bodies and member states. Our role is to operate within these frameworks as an independent civil society actor: translating international obligations into grassroots action, building community capacity to participate in designation processes, and ensuring that the voices of communities are heard in global policy rooms.

As an NGO founded by experts who have worked within the mechanics of these frameworks for decades, we bring an insider understanding of what each designation requires in practice — and the trust of communities who have long been marginalised from the decisions that affect their heritage.
